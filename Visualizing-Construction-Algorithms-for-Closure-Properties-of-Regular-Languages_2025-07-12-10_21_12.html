Abstract

Studying closure properties of regular languages is an integral part of most introductory formal languages and automata theory courses. Students are typically presented with a series of proofs establishing that regular languages are closed under union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection. Each proof formally describes a construction algorithm to build a finite-state machine. For some students, it is difficult to bridge the gap between the formal description of a construction algorithm and the use of the algorithm in practice. To help bridge this gap, many textbooks use graphics to describe construction algorithms. Although helpful, some students still have difficulty carrying out a construction themselves. To further aid students, we have integrated visualization tools for these construction algorithms into FSM-a programming language for the Automata Theory classroom. This article presents five dynamic visualization tools for union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection. Each tool steps through a construction algorithm and automatically creates visually appealing transition-diagram graphics with informative messages illustrating the construction steps. Users may step forward and backwards through any visualization. Empirical data collected from students at a US university and a European university suggests that these are well-received and are useful to understand the construction algorithms.

1 Introduction

In introductory Formal Languages and Automata Theory (FLAT) courses, students are introduced to regular languages, to deterministic and nondeterministic finite-state machines (dfa and ndfa) to decide language membership, and to closure properties of regular languages. Commonly, closure under union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection is studied by exposing students to constructive proofs. Such a proof formally describes a dfa or ndfa machine construction algorithm. Understanding a machine construction algorithm can be challenging for some students in their first FLAT course. The difficulties arise, in part, from their inexperience with formal definitions and statements, thus, creating an environment in which students may not truly understand why the steps of a construction algorithm are necessary. To help students bridge this gap, many textbooks utilize static graphics to illustrate construction steps (e.g., [4,6,9,11−13,20,23] ). Such graphics are useful, but rarely, if ever, completely outline all the steps of a construction algorithm and, therefore, leave some students to fill in the gaps using formal statements they do not (fully) understand.

As is common practice in Computer Science, understanding is promoted by having students implement algorithms as programs. To this end, FSM-a domain-specific language embedded in Racket [1] for the FLAT classroom [16, 19]-has been developed. In FSM, students program state machines [14,16] and visualize the execution of the machines they implement [18,19] . Both of these technologies, assist students in understanding the formalisms presented to them for designing a machine from scratch. They do not, however, help students understand the formalisms nor the algorithms developed to understand closure properties of regular languages. To further help students, we have developed 5 dynamic visualization tools to understand why regular languages are closed under union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection. The tools are integrated into FSM and are inspired by the graphics found in FLAT textbooks. Each tool allows the user to step forward and backwards through a construction algorithm. At each step, a visually appealing graphic for the current approximation of the result's transition diagram is automatically rendered along with informative messages explaining the step taken. It is important for visualization tools to have low extraneous cognitive load in order to enhance learning [5,7,24] . To this end,our tools provide an easy-to-use interface, helpful informative messages, and the use of color to understand the transition from one step to the next.

This article describes the visualization tools and presents empirical data collected from students at two universities: one in the US and one in Europe. The collected data strongly suggests that the tools are well-received and useful. The article is organized as follows. Section 2 briefly introduces FSM. Section 3 describes the visualization tools. Section 4 summarizes the quantitative and qualitative data collected. Section 5 briefly reviews and contrasts with related work. Finally, Section 6 presents concluding remarks and directions for future work.

2 Brief Introduction to FSM

2.1 Core Definitions

The machine constructors of interest for this article are those for finite-state automatons:

make-dfa: K∑SFδ→dfa

make-ndfa: K∑SFδ→ndfa

K denotes the states, ∑ denotes the input alphabet, S∈K denotes the starting state, F⊆K denotes the final states,and δ denotes a transition relation, which must be a function for a dfa. A dfa transition rule is a triple, (K∑K) ,containing a source state,the element read,and a destination state. An ndfa transition rule is a triple, (K{∑∪ϵ} K), containing a source state, the element to read (possibly none), and a destination state.

There is an observer for each component of a finite state machine. For instance,(sm - statesM)evaluates to M ’s list of states and (sm-rules M) evaluates to M's list of transition rules. There are two observers to apply a machine to a word: (sm-apply M w) and (sm-showtransitions M w). The first returns the result of the application: ' accept or ' reject. The second returns a trace of the machine configurations traversed ending with the result. A trace is only returned, however, if the machine is a dfa or if the word is accepted by an ndfa.

Integrated into FSM are also two visualization tools: sm-graph and sm-visualize. The first returns a graphic for the transition diagram of a given machine. The second is a dynamic visualization tool that allows the user to step through the execution of applying a given machine to a given word. Similar to sm-showtransitions, the dynamic visualization tool only produces a visualization for dfa execution or an accepting ndfa execution.

To illustrate programming finite-state machines, consider implementing an ndfa to decide L={ab∗∪ba∗} . The FSM implementation is displayed in Figure 1. In the starting state, S, the machine nondeterministically decides if the given word is in ab∗ or in ba∗ and transitions, respectively, to A or D. In A, to accept, the machine must read an a and move to B to read an arbitrary number of b. In D, to accept, the machine must read a b and move to E to read an arbitrary number of a. If the machine is unable to read the elements of the given word in this manner then it halts and rejects. Observe that nondeterminism (e.g., the transitions out of S) is a language feature that programmers may use. Finally, the unit tests illustrate words that are and that are not in L (lines 8-9) and assist both students and instructors to understand how well the given machine works.

3 Dynamic Visualizations

3.1 Overall Design

FSM dynamic visualizations are divided into three, vertically stacked, components: main visualization graphic, informative messages, and the instructions. The main visualization graphic illustrates an algorithm in a stepwise manner. The informative messages provide context for the step displayed. Their content is carefully tailored to make the algorithm comprehensible to all students.

The instructions indicate how to interact with the visualization. The list of instructions is specified as follows:

↑ Moves the visualization to the start

→ Moves the visualization one step forward

← Moves the visualization one step backwards

↓ Moves the visualization to the end

W Zoom in 10%

S Zoom out 10%

R Zoom to fit the main visualization image in the visualization's frame

E Zoom to main visualization image's original dimensions

F Zoom to the maximum factor

  • Click and hold to drag an image

The goal of this simple set of instructions is to provide a low extraneous cognitive load for students. In this manner, instead of focusing on learning how to use the tools, they can focus on the nuances of the construction algorithms.

3.2 Union

For the union visualization,assume M and N are defined as follows: M=(make-ndfa:KM∑MSMFMδM) and N=( make-ndfa: KN ∑NSNFNδN ) To simplify the discussion,assume that KM∩KN=∅

The constructor for union creates a new machine MU that has the following characteristics:

  • A new starting state, SU such that SU∉{KM∪KN}

  • KU={SU}∪KM∪KN∑U=∑M∪∑N

  • FU=FM∪FN

  • δU={(SUϵSM)(SUϵSN)}∪δM∪δN

To the readers of this article, the algorithm described presents no cognitive challenge. To a student inexperienced with formal notation, however, the collection of symbols and equations do not clearly communicate the algorithm.

To illustrate the visualization tool, consider the machines defined in Figure 2. Figure 3 displays the initial visualization image after invoking the union tool. It displays the transition diagrams for both given machines. The edges of each machine are highlighted in different colors allowing the user to easily distinguish which machine an edge belongs to in the next step of the visualization. This next step, displayed in Figure 4a, creates a graphic for the union machine as generated by the constructor outlined above. In this graphic, SU=C ,the ϵ -transitions out of C are rendered in black, and the transitions from the given machines retain their color highlighting. An informative message highlights the salient features of the construction. The color scheme and the informative message are used to reduce the extraneous cognitive load for students by allowing them to more easily understand the components of the constructed machine.

3.3 Concatenation

Given two machines, M and N ,the concatenation constructor creates a new machine MC that has the following characteristics:

  • SC=SM

  • ∑C=∑M∪∑N

  • FC=FN

⋅KC=KM∪KN

  • δC={{(fM∈SN)∣fM∈FM}∪δM∪δN}

Invariably, a significant number of students have difficulty understanding the last equation: new empty transitions need to be created from every state in FM to SN . That is,students feel uncomfortable with set formation notation.

To illustrate the visualization tool, once again, consider the two machines in Figure 2. When the user runs the concatenation visualization, the first image is the same as for union-viz as displayed in Figure 3. Note that for concat-viz the order of the arguments matters given that concatenation is not commutative. The next step in the concatenation visualization is illustrated in Figure 4b. The ϵ -transitions from the states in FM into SN are rendered in black and the edges of the input machines retain their color coding to make the resulting transition diagram easier to read and relate to the input machines. Observe that the informative message highlights the starting and final states of the constructed machine along with the edges generated.

3.4 Kleene Star

For the Kleene Star visualization, assume M is defined as follows:

M= (make-ndfa: KM∑MSMFMδM )

The constructor for Kleene star creates a new machine MK that has the following characteristics:

  • A new starting state, SK such that SK∉KM

  • ∑K=∑M

  • FK={SK}∪FM

  • KK={SK}∪KM

  • δK={{(SK∈SM)}∪{(fM∈SK)∣fM∈FM}∪δM}

Once again, the set formation notation presents a challenge for many students.

To illustrate the Kleene star visualization tool, consider invoking it with aa*-ab*-e from Figure 2. The first image displays the given ndfa with all its edges highlighted in violet as displayed in Figure 5a. The next step illustrates the result as displayed in Figure 5b. The new ϵ -transitions are rendered in black and the given machine’s edges retain their color. All the salient features are highlighted in the informative messages: the new starting state and the new transitions generated.

3.5 Complement

For the complement visualization, assume M is defined as follows:

M= (make-ndfa: KM∑MSMFMδM) 

L(M) ’s complement is defined as L―(M)={w∣w∉L(M)} . The construction for the machine that decides L―(M) requires, MD ,a dfa that decides the same language as M . This means that the given machine may first have to be converted to dfa. For our students, this step usually is not an obstacle given that such a construction algorithm has been previously studied in the course using the visualization developed by Morazán and Minić [17]. If M is a dfa then MD is M . Otherwise,it is the result of converting M into a dfa. We denote MD as follows:

3.6 Intersection

Given two ndfas, M and N ,the intersection constructor creates a new machine, MI ,based on the following definition for set intersection:

L(M)∩L(N)=L(M)∪L(N)―

Students are explained that the equation outlines the steps required to build MI . First,the complements of M and N are needed. The construction of each of these machines, as discussed in Section 3.5, may require a transformation into a dfa. Second, the union of the two complement machines is required. It is emphasized to students that this step may require renaming the states of one of the input machines to guarantee that the intersection of their states is empty. Finally,the complement of this union machine yields MI . This final step requires, as outlined in Section 3.5, converting the generated union machine into a dfa.

To illustrate the intersection visualization tool, consider using aa*-ab*-e and aab* from Figure 2. The visualization renders graphics for each machine constructed as outlined above. That is, it includes graphics illustrating any conversion into a dfa, the result of renaming the states of a machine, the result of constructing a union machine, and the result of constructing any complement machine. In the interest of brevity, we do not display all these graphics. Figure 7a displays the graphic for the union of the complements of the input machines. The final step of the visualization, after converting the machine displayed in Figure 7a to a dfa, is displayed in Figure 7b . The informative message indicates to the user that the step takes the complement of converting the machine in Figure 7a into a dfa. This is conveyed using a machine name defined in a previous visualization message. Observe that the machine only accepts (a a), which is the only word in the intersection of the languages of the given input machines.

4 Empirical Study

To evaluate the usefulness of the tools, we present empirical data collected from students taking their first FLAT course at two universities: 18 from Seton Hall University and 11 from Instituto Univer-sitario de Lisboa. During the course module on closure properties of regular languages, students were taught about the constructive algorithms in lectures, were exposed to our tools, and were given time to experiment with them. Respondents did not receive compensation nor benefits for participating in the survey.

Upon completion of the module, students were presented with a set of statements to measure if they found each tool useful to understand the corresponding construction algorithm. These responses were measured on a Likert scale [10] from 1 (Strongly disagree) to 5 (Strongly agree), with 3 as a neutral response. The statements presented are:

S1.1-5 I find this visualization helpful to understand the construction algorithm for union/concatenation/ Kleene star/ complement/intersection.

S2.1-5 I find the informative messages helpful to understand the construction algorithm for union/concatenation/ Kleene star/ complement/intersection.

The distribution of responses for the S1 is displayed in Figure 8a. The overwhelming majority of respondents,over 90% for all statements, tend to agree (i.e., responses 4 or 5). This is a very positive result that strongly suggests that students find the visualizations helpful. The distribution of responses for the S2 statements is displayed in Figure 8b. We observe that for all statements an overwhelming majority of respondents, 89% or more, tend to agree (i.e., responses 4 or 5 ). The responses strongly affirm that the informative messages are helpful for students navigating the visualizations.

In addition, for each tool, we collected answers to open-ended questions: What did you find most useful about the tool? and What can be improved about the tool?. Among the most useful characteristics mentioned are ease of use, the ability to follow along, the use of color, and the informative message. Sample responses are:

The colors and being able to follow along the visualization

Being able to visualize and see what I am working on step by step

Being able to follow along with the generated edges

The color highlighting of the different machines is the most useful

I found the tools very helpful in understanding the concepts. I first saw them in class while covering the material and the visualization helped in cementing the material

The most common answer regarding improvements suggested no changes. Responses suggesting changes referred to improving the use of color. Sample responses are:

I would make final states a separate color, like red perhaps, so it stands out more.

What each color means needs to be clearly defined

Need to add more colors, everything is clustered and hard to read [regarding the intersection tool]

The first response suggests that representing final states differently (i.e., as double circles) may be furthered improved by rendering them in color (as done for the starting state in green). The second response suggests that instructors may need to do a better job at explaining the use of color in each of the tools and that the addition of a color legend may be helpful. The final response requires a great deal of reflection. A machine resulting from intersection tends to be large (i.e., has many states and edges) and is the result of several transformations as the construction advances. Currently, it is unclear to us how color may be used to help track the transformations performed.

In summary, the quantitative and qualitative results suggest that the tools are well-received by students and useful to teach constructive algorithms for closure properties of regular languages. The coloring scheme and informative messages are helpful to clarify and provide any additional context to better understand the algorithms. The improvements the students have suggested are part of our immediate future work.

5 Related Work

Many textbooks present the closure of regular languages under union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection without the benefit of a programming language that allows students to experiment with writing and using the construction algorithms discussed [4,6,9,11,13,20,23] . To make formal statements easier to understand, most textbooks use static graphics to illustrate how some constructors work. The only textbook that is tightly-coupled with a programming language uses FSM [15]. This textbook has its readers implement the constructors. Readers can use their constructors to build new machines, test these machines, and examine the transition diagrams of said machines. This textbook, as others, also uses static graphics to help explain the construction algorithms. The work presented in this article takes the best practices of both these approaches. It integrates into FSM the use of graphics to help students understand the construction algorithms. Unlike the static approach taken by textbooks, the work presented in this article uses a graphical dynamic visualization to explain construction algorithms. By making the graphics dynamic, users can step forward and backwards during the construction steps to better understand the construction algorithms.

The most widely-used graphical system in FLAT courses is JFLAP [21,22] . In JFLAP,students must manually layout the transition diagram of their machines. The study of construction algorithms for closure properties is also done by manually creating nodes and edges [8]. That is, students must create the nodes and the edges the construction algorithm requires. In addition, students must place them in an appealing manner that makes it easy to understand the machine's transition diagram. Such manual construction may help some students better understand the construction algorithms, but is frustrating when, for example, the formal notation describing an algorithm is not understood. In contrast, the presented work has nodes and edges automatically created as the student advances the dynamic visualization. To make the layout appealing, the work presented uses GraphViz [2,3] . A construction algorithm’s result is built by steps. At each step, a new transition diagram, including new nodes and edges, is automatically rendered as a graphic. In this manner, students can fully focus on the steps of a construction algorithm and not be distracted by creating an appealing graphic.

6 Concluding Remarks

This article presents five novel dynamic visualization tools to help FLAT students understand the construction algorithms that establish that regular languages are closed under union, concatenation, Kleene star, complement, and intersection. These visualizations present an easy-to-use interface that imposes a low extraneous cognitive load on students. Each visualization tool automatically produces appealing graphics for each step of a construction algorithm. Student understanding is fostered by allowing movement forward and backwards through the visualization. In addition, color coding and informative messages are used to help explain the salient features of every step. Empirical data collected from students in a course using the described tools strongly suggests that the visualization tools are well-received and useful.

Future work includes performing a more in-depth empirical study across several offerings of the course. In addition, we aim to conduct studies across several universities to have a more diverse sample. Building on the success of the described tools, future work also entails developing visualization tools for word derivation using regular, context-free, and context-sensitive grammars. The first two shall help students visualize the construction of derivation trees whilst the later shall help students visualize the construction of derivation graphs. References
