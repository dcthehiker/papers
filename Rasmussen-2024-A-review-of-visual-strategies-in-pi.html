ABSTRACT

This pictorial review illustrates an array of visual strategies employed in 271 pictorials published in DIS, C&C, and TEI conferences between 2014 and 2023. Based on a thematic analysis the pictorial review presents the visual strategies under seven analytical themes: 1) composition, 2) typography, 3) colour, 4) drawing attention, 5) visual style, 6) active engagement, and 7) visual related work. The review focuses on strategies for communicating research visually, rather than the content and contribution of the reviewed pictorials. The review outlines both basic graphical principles, as well as novel use of visual elements. The pictorial is the first attempt at a visual analysis of the pictorial format, offering authors of future pictorials, both an overview of the development of the format, as well as inspiration for new ways to complement text with visual content. Lastly, the challenges and future potentials which emerged from the review are discussed.

INTRODUCTION

Design has a long history of using illustrations and graphical elements to convey knowledge through portfolios and presentations. This approach has been taken up by Interaction Design and Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), through annotated portfolios [39,61,89], pictorials (e.g. [35]), as well as other work that rely on imagery to convey design research knowledge [13]. The pictorial format emerged as a result of a CHI 2012 workshop [13] that explored visual literacy and visual thinking in HCI. The first Pictorial Track arose at the Designing Interactive Systems (DIS) conference in 2014 and spread to Creativity & Cognition (C&C) in 2019, and Tangible, Embedded, and Embodied Interaction (TEI) in 2020. Since 2020 pictorials have also had some inclusion at for example MobileHCI, Interaction Design with Children (IDC) and NordiCHI. The visual format of pictorials have evolved over the years, from the first publications that generally adhered strictly to the pictorial template (e.g. [92,108]), to a more free and explorative use of the format (e.g. [35,101]). Creative layout choices is supported in the pictorial template by stating "the Pictorials track encourages and supports authors’ creativity and design choices’ (p.1 [115]) and "authors can change the fonts and formatting styles used’ (p.1 [115]).
This visual review is motivated by the author's use of the pictorial format as part of teaching several interaction design courses at Aarhus University, Computer Science. Students found it challenging to understand the visual possibilities of the format when asked to present their design work as a pictorial. Presently, there are few reflections on pictorials, which can serve as a source of inspiration and understanding of the format, apart from the work by Rutkowska et al. [97] who analysed 51 pictorials and identified six strategies for communicating design knowledge in pictorials, and Peeters et al. [86] who reflected on different visual strategies for the dissemination of research through active perception. The aim of this pictorial is to complement these works, by focusing on how, rather than what design knowledge is communicated. Consequently, the review does not engage with the contribution of the reviewed pictorials but instead focuses on the visual means employed, thereby providing a visual resource of graphical strategies. It is important to note that the review should not be considered an exhaustive or prescriptive list of what pictorials should look like but be seen as an introduction to the current use of the format, which can hopefully inspire and encourage even more creative use of the pictorial format in the future. This pictorial review explores the visual strategies employed to convey ideas and contributions in pictorials published at the three main and more stabilized pictorial venues, DIS, C&C ,and TEI. The authors of this pictorial are designers and have experience in graphical design for print and screen (authors 1 and 2 are industrial designers and author 3 is a service designer and IT product developer).
We conducted a thematic analysis [17] of 271 pictorials published between 2014 to 2023. In preparation for the analysis, each researcher went through the reviewed pictorials and collected representative screenshots of pages with different visual strategies. All screenshots were uploaded to a Miro board (an online visual collaboration platform), and the screenshot of page one has a link to the full pictorial in ACM Digital Library (DL). Then the screenshots were annotated with post-its, highlighting either the type of content, the visual style, or questions raised, as well as good and bad visual practices. Each pictorial was gone through by at least two out of the three authors.
Based on the collected screenshots and annotations 10 themes were identified, each theme was further detailed by revisiting the collected screenshots and identifying further examples. To complement the bottom-up approach and to develop and refine sub-themes, the analysis was also reviewed through a top-down approach, using established graphical principles. During the writing process, the themes were further refined into the seven themes presented in this pictorial. This refinement has also been impacted by insightful comments from anonymous reviewers during the review process. The themes are presented in this pictorial, in the same order as displayed in the "refine themes" stage, starting with composition, and ending with visual-related work.
While the Miro board was used primarily to collaborate throughout the analysis and writing process, it also served a secondary purpose as a visual resource for locating relevant and interesting pictorials for projects and teaching. COMPOSITION

Most pictorial compositions have a clear reading order, making it effortless for the reader to follow the story along the page, despite a varied use of column lengths and placements. However, when departing from the Western tradition of top-left-to-bottom-right reading pattern [54] and orderly column-row arrangements, it may result in layouts with unclear reading order (e.g. [52]) - leaving the reader uncertain where to continue. While this might be intentional, coloured areas (e.g. [82]), or other visual elements, such as lines or arrows (e.g. [52]) can be used to direct the reader's attention across the page.
Pictorials have displayed a wide variety of layouts, where the vast majority utilize a grid, more or less strictly, to structure the elements on the page. Pages are often organised in two [74], three (according to the new ACM template in columns of equal width [28] or the old ACM template in one narrow and two wider columns [95]), or four columns [11,42]. However, image-heavy pages are in some cases also organised in five [44] or even six [32] columns (see Typography section on the next page for readability implications of column width). Authors also often vary the number of columns in the different pages of the pictorial (e.g. [66,84]). Grids support creating visual structure both vertically and horizontally across the page while allowing for flexibility in placement and size of elements. The grid can be used to create images of equal weight [11] or to build a visual hierarchy [4] with images of varying sizes (e.g. [95]), and can be accentuated using justified text [11]. A few pictorials depart from the grid and experiment with more dynamic layout, such as placing images in an irregular pattern (e.g. [9]), varying sizes (e.g. [41]), or altogether breaking the grid, placing both images and text at different angles [35] creating a more chaotic page composition. Breaking with the grid can be visually effective and engaging to read. However, doing so may challenge readability for both sighted and visually impaired readers.
Creating a harmonious layout depends not only on the consistent placement of images but also on keeping a consistent spacing between them [65], using a grid-based layout can help to avoid mixed spacing [95]. When composing the pictorial layouts, the vast majority of authors utilize almost all available space on a page (including this pictorial), leaving very little blank space - apart from margins and spacing between elements (e.g. [21]). However, rather than using every available square cm ,authors might consider choosing fewer elements and leaving areas of white space on the page (e.g. [27,92]) ,thereby drawing attention to the central content.

TYPOGRAPHY

The majority of pictorials follow the typography suggestion of the ACM template and use a serif font for the body text (e.g. [77]), while some depart from this and use sans serif font instead [40,83] ),either on all pages (e.g. [69]),or on all apart from the first page (e.g. [33]). Choosing different fonts for the pictorial can support setting a specific tone for the story, such as underlining a comic style format [33], or letting the typography match the visuals in the pictorial [35]. Pictorials can also employ a different font to indicate places where the author speaks directly to the reader (often in a handwritten font), commenting on the work [45], inviting for action [45], or asking the reader questions [87].
Body text generally uses left alignment (e.g. [99]), which is easier to read, as the varying line length helps the reader to keep track of where they are, or justified text which creates a neater and more defined layout (e.g. [55]). Body text can also be used creatively as part of the visual storytelling, for example, to illustrate the limits of wi-fi signals by impacting the text's line length and alignment [90], or to link text closely to parts of a diagram [86]. Ragged lines on both the left and right edge, such as centre-aligned text or text in creative shapes, work well for short paragraphs but can challenge readability when used in longer sections of text (e.g. [86]). When authors diverge from the ACM pictorial template layout, the readability of line length is often disregarded. The optimal line length for readability of continuous text is 45-75 characters per line [26], equivalent to a suggested three-column format in the ACM pictorial template (50-60 characters per line). Both longer (e.g. [50]) or shorter lines (e.g. [78]) can disrupt the reading flow, either by making it difficult to travel back and forth across a long line or breaking it through repeated line changes. However, the recommendation should only be treated as a guideline and authors are free to experiment with line length if it serves a purpose. Placing text over images can create compelling visuals, but the contrast between text and background can also challenge readability (e.g. [9]). Several pictorials have shown how contrast can be achieved, for example by using a coloured box behind the text to provide contrast [6], or by toning down the image with an overlay [101].
In pictorials, typography has also been used as a visual element going beyond simply using a font, for example using added visuals to illustrate a word in the title [35], elaborate graphical details to headings to match the style of the paper [35], or used emojis in the title and a description in brackets to make it searchable as well as accessible [91]. THEME SCHEME EMPHASISING
While colour elsewhere in graphic design is given a great deal of attention, pictorial authors often rely on colour themes resulting from the chosen images. However, choosing an intentional colour theme can set a specific tone or atmosphere as some examples (e.g. [35,48]) have illustrated.
Considering the type of colour scheme used, the theory of colour harmony [56] offers strategies for choosing colours based on a fixed relationship in the colour wheel. A monochromatic colour scheme is easy to implement as it only relies on tones, shades and tints within a specific hue (e.g. [27]), but in the absence of multiple colours - texture, form, and other design elements, can help elements stand out. A similar approach can be found in analogous colour schemes, which are formed by (three) adjacent colours (e.g. [14]). While both of these colour strategies create harmonious colour schemes, a challenge can be their low contrast between colours. Complementary colour schemes on the other hand are created by combining colours from opposite sides of the colour wheel (e.g. [106]), creating a very high contrast colour scheme. These are rarely seen in pictorials, but Treusch et al. [106] illustrate how effective they can be at highlighting the content in focus by contrasting the red knit on the green background, taming the bold contrast by considering contrast of extension [59], limiting the proportion of one colour relative to the other. Triadic and square colour schemes are used to pick colour palettes with high contrast while seeking to balance diversity and harmony. Triadic colour schemes are created by three hues equally spaced around the colour wheel (e.g. [83]), while square colour schemes are formed by four (e.g. [102]).
Using colour to emphasise aspects has been done through colour coding, which provides a way to create visual links between textual and visual content (e.g. [2]), to group ideas [49], connecting findings between different participants [46], or showing different design aspects or steps [83]. When using colour coding the distinctions must be clear, for example, while the green and orange colours used for colour coding in [49] seem very distinct in normal vision, to some types of colour blindness they closely resemble one another. Tools such as [116,117] make it possible to check whether the chosen colours are distinct to people with the most common colour blindness (i.e. red-green). Colour has also been used to highlight textual paragraphs, colouring a portion of the text, such as seen in [82] or to create a graphical visual style that flows across the pages of the pictorial [27], which makes it easy for the reader to catch the main points at a glance.

ANNOTATIONS HIGHLIGHTING TEXT

DRAWING ATTENTION

Authors have used both visual and textual annotations to draw attention to essential aspects of text, figures, and images. Textual annotations have often been made using a hand-written front, for example, to illustrate a storyboard [95], reveal design knowledge, ideas [73], data analysis insights [22], or allow the author to pose questions directly to the reader [87]. However, a few examples have chosen to use a computer-style font, either similar to the one used in the body text or headings (e.g. [23,95]) or a different font that supports the visual content [38]. Similarly, visual annotations have been used for highlighting key details through coloured overlay dots (e.g. [76]), process doodles (e.g. [85]), icons and interaction gestures (e.g. [1]), or by circulating the content (e.g. [60]). Callouts have been used for linking the annotations to the specific parts of an image or illustration through lines connecting elements on the page (e.g. text and figures [14,43] or figures and figures [83,113] ),or circles to reveal close-ups (e.g. [69,113]) and x-ray images of non-visible parts of the object (e.g. [96,111]). Well-crafted annotations can serve as visual arguments and provide a way for the authors to highlight important aspects. The tone of the annotated argument can be tuned. A clean graphic line can give the annotation a formal tone, while a sketchy style or handwritten font adds a more informal [45] or personal touch, providing insight into the author's thoughts during the analysis process [37].
Authors have drawn attention to elements by adding icons connecting content across pages (e.g. [51,74,100]), or separating different types of content on the page, such as user comments (e.g. [100]) or parts of a design space (e.g. [94]). Icons have also been used as a visual guide for the reader to which part of the design process they are reading about (e.g. [27]), or link the discussion section to presented concept ideas [16]. Icons can serve as a strong visual identification that allows the reader to easily grasp connections between different elements in the pictorial, and such connections can be enhanced by the use of colour coding to group and connect across different icons (e.g. [1,94]).
The ACM pictorial template does not give any recommendations on highlighting text, but standard practice in publications is either to use bold or italic font. Pictorials, often depart from the standard practice, in favour of using colour. Such as using a coloured font to highlight different words (e.g. [24]), a coloured area behind the text (e.g. [8,31,82,102]), or by placing an image element behind, such as a hand-sketched highlighter [105]. Each of these approaches can both support the chosen visual style and highlight content in a more expressive manner.

VISUAL STYLE

The majority of the reviewed pictorials rely on a more or less classical paper look, keeping relatively close to the pictorial template, in terms of font and layout, either the old ACM extended abstract template with two wider and one narrower column, or the newer ACM template with three equally wide columns, and then playing around with the placement of images (e.g. [77,102]). However, in recent years many pictorials have taken up the encouragement to use the format creatively, and begun to experiment with more explorative visual styles, thereby disregarding the template to a greater or lesser extent, at least after the first page (e.g. [35]). In some cases, the visual style cuts across all pages (e.g. [8,35] ),while in others only on a few of the pages, with the rest having a more classical paper look (e.g. [101]). Some pictorials (e.g. [8,29,101]) have chosen a visual style resembling magazines, creating pages with full-page images and info-style boxes [101,109] decorative visual elements and pull-out quotes in different fonts (e.g. [8]). A few pictorials have crafted layouts with inspiration from collages [35,46] ,creating layouts that mix different fonts,and create the appearance of elements that are cut out and pasted on the page, often with jagged edges to the cut-out, and in many cases at slight angles as if they were quickly pinned up. The result of the layout style is a playful and lively take on a pictorial, far removed from the traditional formal visual style of the ACM template - and more like a view into the authors' notebook. Few pictorials also draw their style inspiration from books, such as the pictorial 'Qualities of Focus' [12], which illustrates just how far the pictorial format can be taken. In the pictorial, nine out of the twelve pages are completely devoid of written content, making it seem more like an art book than an academic paper Another example plays on the visual format of a cookbook [62]. In such image-heavy visual styles, alt text can provide a possibility for a clean layout, while providing a hidden space for the authors' comments (e.g. [36,105]). A few pictorials use a comic book style (e.g. [15,33]) transforming the academic content into a visual story. While some comic book examples keep the horizontal page layout [15] - one breaks with the standard page orientation of pictorials by turning the page vertically to mimic the classic format of comics [33]. Comics format gives priority to visual storytelling and allows for a strong integration between text and images.
The pictorial format holds the potential for exploring many other visual styles than the ones listed here, perhaps the future will bring a pictorial in the style of infographics or an interactive version using motion and dynamic elements inspired by for example [47,67] .

ACTIVE ENGAGEMENT

A few pictorials have encouraged readers to grasp parts of their pictorial contribution by having them actively engage in understanding the material. Engagement spans from experiencing dynamic content through scanning a QR code to access either GIFs [10], video content [75], or a soundtrack [64].
A higher level of active engagement is found in examples where the reader is involved in active knowledge construction, through for example finding and circling the pictorial's keywords [87] or by connecting the dots from motion capture to explore and reflect upon dancers' movements [87]. Another example can be seen in Peeters et al. [88], where readers have to construct a paper model to understand how the system works [63,88] . While these examples do not convey the full extent of the prototype or pattern, the active engagement serves as a valuable way to support readers in gaining a more reflective understanding of the presented work, through going beyond simply reading about it.
Good research publications allow others to test or continue the presented work, and some pictorials (e.g. [2,45,66]) make it easy for readers to create their own versions of the presented material or apply the research disseminated in their projects. Such as for example by printing, cutting and making a kirigami pattern directly from the printed pictorial [63,66] ,printing a handbook containing DIY instructions that they can place on their shelf [30], using the method cards from the pictorial [20,72] ,or opening the pdf in Illustrator and thereby turning a stitch patterns in the pdf into vector lines that can be used to recreate and further explore the patterns presented [113]. Exploration of active engagement has also been done through alternative use of ALT text, which allows the reader to discover a hidden layer of information in the document, where for example the authors' reflections and feelings can be uncovered when the mouse is hovered over the images [36].
Providing the user with a choice of their own path through the pictorial offers a different way to engage, such as in the work by Yoo et al. [112] where a diagram is used to indicate alternative ways to read the pictorial, for example, choosing to read sections 3,6,7,8 if the reader is simply interested in the Envisioning Cards Toolkit presented - inspiring the reader to dynamically reconfigure their pathway. The example presents one approach to support abstracts and introductions with a visual counterpart, as these sections historically have been predominantly textual.

VISUAL RELATED WORK

When writing academic papers in general, the related work section plays a central component - positioning the work in relation to what already exists, highlighting key similarities and differences. In the pictorial format, visual elements could potentially play a significant role in communicating salient points about key related work. However, this is not the case, related work in pictorials is almost exclusively textual (e.g. [7,70] ),and in many cases,pictorials do not even include a full related work section but simply have an (extended) introduction section (e.g. [18,93]).
One alternative to creating a visual representation for the related work section is to visually connect the related work to different parts of an image of the presented work in the pictorial, for example, a prototype, allowing the reader to see how existing knowledge links to new knowledge [1]. Out of the 271 pictorials reviewed, only a handful support the related work with visual materials. Most of these examples use images of key references to illustrate key examples of related work (e.g. [87,98]), allowing readers unfamiliar with the cited work to easily compare and contrast them. Other examples venture further by creating a graphical synthesis through diagrams to showcase the relationship between different images of related work, for example, through set diagrams sorting examples into two visual categories [34], or through a Venn-style diagram with illustrations of three different related products, making their mutual relationship evident [53]. Few pictorials [66,104] use visual representations to structure and contribute knowledge, akin to how the text is traditionally used to create categories and compare related work. The chart-like visual representation of the related work, by Kaspersen et al. [66] presents the related work in a diagrammatic form showing different design aspects of the cited work and how they relate. Strengers et al. [104] also uses a diagrammatic representation of the related work to show clusters of related work, and how the work presented in the pictorial fills gaps in the existing literature. The visual elements in both of these examples go beyond what is presented in the text, enabling readers to explore the illustrations and gain additional information. Thereby living up to the overall endeavour of pictorial to make the visual material a primary means of conveying information with at least, if not more, importance than the accompanying text.
While visual representations of related work in pictorials are uncommon, the few existing examples have demonstrated the potential, and hint at the possibility for future pictorials, which may reveal even more ways to also allow the visual material equal weight in relation to text, when composing related work sections.
DISCUSSION
In the following, we will discuss the challenges and future possibilities for pictorials that arose from the review.

VISUAL CONTENT THROUGHOUT?

Reviewing the 271 pictorials revealed numerous visually interesting examples, showcasing everything from user insights (e.g. [46]), to ideation (e.g. [3]), design process (e.g. [110]) or design documentation (e.g. [84]) and beyond. However, the same cannot be said for the introduction, related work, discussion, and conclusion, where despite the high number of pictorials reviewed, there are very few examples (e.g. [16,34,66] ) that use visual material to support the text. As shown in this review, visual material can be used throughout the whole pictorial to complement the written content. While supplementing the introduction, discussion, and conclusion with visual arguments and descriptions might not always be straightforward, it is much easier to imagine what visual material might supplement the text in the related work section. While a visual supplement to the related work seems obvious, the approach is challenged by image licensing, seeking permission from authors and attributing the source of the image, or the effort and time to create illustrations of the referenced work (e.g. [19,81]). However, based on the review is seems a worthwhile endeavour for the future of pictorials to expand the sections that are visually supplemented, as these sections could equally benefit from using visual material to support the reader in unpacking, comparing, or reflecting on the content.

READING ORDER AND ACCESSIBILITY

One of the appeals and strengths of the pictorial format is the possibility to break free from the strict and linear format of the ACM paper template and be able to freely choose the desired typography, page layout, size and placement of columns and images, as well as the extensive use of visual imagery. However, such freedom also comes with the responsibility to create a good reading experience, regardless of whether the reader is sighted or has a visual impairment. Some authors, such as Byrne et al. [21], intentionally challenges the standard good practices for layout, and the authors even end the pictorial by explaining that "regular shifts in aesthetic, arrangement, and tone are deliberate and aim to further unsettle the reading of this paper" (p. 770 [21]) and thank the reader for indulging them. The example illustrates that the basic graphical principles that form the basis of this review does not always have to be followed, but can be (successfully) broken, as long as the author does so intentionally and for a reason arising from the topic of the pictorial.
Overall, the reviewed pictorials (despite their sometimes rather experimental layout) are very successful in supporting the reader in moving across the page and seeing the content in a logical manner. However, when it comes to supporting accessible reading order a lot of the pictorials fail. Even though they include alt text for images describing their content, the reading order is often not optimal or resembles that of a sighted person. Creating ALT text and ensuring reading order is a much more time-consuming and cumbersome task for pictorials than papers. While ensuring readability of pictorials to all readers, both sighted and visually impaired, it might be fruitful to consider ways to lighten the load on pictorial authors. While best practices for ALT text is generally to describe images individually, the workload of authors might be lightened significantly, by considering whether each image holds separate content (e.g. [94]) or images on the page are connected, showing variations of the same thing (e.g. [68]). In the latter case providing ALT text for a group of images might make more sense (and allowable within ALT text guidelines [58]), as well as potentially improve the reading experience of non-sighted readers, by making it more similar to the sighted reader, by avoiding repetition, in favour of descriptions summarizing the main theme, purpose, or content of the images collectively. The reviewed pictorials also illustrated that ALT text can offer a space for creative exploration, providing a hidden layer of information that can be discovered by the reader (e.g. [36]). While such creative use of ALT text can offer all readers novel information, the challenge is that the information might not always be visible in some page readers, such as Preview or the ACM DL e-reader.

THE CHALLENGE OF ENGAGEMENT

In this pictorial we have highlighted examples of ways to shift people from passive readers to active knowledge con-structers [87,88] ,or support them in expanding upon the presented work [30,45,66]) . The approach potentially allows the reader a closer connection with the presented research. However, it also raises the question of how many times a reader actually has taken the time to download, print, cut, fold, or draw - to actively engage with the material? VISUAL KINSHIP AND ANONYMISATION

A challenge that emerged during the review process, pertains to anonymization in pictorials. Going beyond the conventional anonymization practices applied to authorship and self-citations in papers, the graphical and visual elements, such as style, illustration type, colour theme, and even font choice, can potentially indicate or even reveal the author's identity, group affiliation, or project relationship (e.g. [71,79,82]). All designers have their own visualisation and drawing style allowing designers to separate from each other. However, the hand drawing style is not as unique as the handwriting style of the person, as shared educational and cultural context affects it. Personal drawing (e.g. [36,37]) or visualisation style [e.g. [71,79,82] can, however, reveal at least one of the authors of the pictorial. While the same challenge pertains to papers, the extent of images and the degree of focus on them is different in pictorials, making the challenge more apparent in this context. However, by indicating this challenge, it is not our intention to pose further restrictions on authors, but simply to highlight it both as a challenge and quality. In the review process, complete anonymization is difficult, however, it raises a challenge as authors with very distinct visualisation or drawing styles are more likely to be identified. On the other hand, strong identifying styles is also a quality, as it can create a visual kinship between pictorials that can help readers connect projects or authors simply based on their visual style.

LOCATING PICTORIALS

Presently, pictorials are catalogued in the ACM DL in a way that makes them indistinguishable from papers. This signals pictorials as equal to papers and ensures that they cannot (easily) be excluded from reviews. While we agree that pictorials should be considered equal to papers and support the move to abandon specific pictorial tracks at conferences, in favour of creating conference tracks determined based on the content, rather than publication format. Apart from the 11 pictorials that have been tagged with "best pictorial" and can be located by filtering for "paper award" and then "best pictorial", it is impossible to find any other pictorials. There are also more awarded pictorials, but they have not been recorded in the ACM DL. Consequently, pictorials have to be located manually from conference proceedings, or through searches (which also produces a range of irrelevant papers). This process makes it difficult to conduct reviews, but perhaps more importantly, it also hinders the opportunity for authors to get inspiration beyond the few examples listed on the conference web pages. While the goal of this pictorial has not been to address this challenge, the Miro board created to aid our analysis has inadvertently partly addressed it. While writing this pictorial we found that the board served a secondary, but maybe equally important purpose, by providing us a convenient and very visual way to explore pictorials through example pages and annotations when discussing different ways to visually communicate research with students and colleagues. We hope that this pictorial and the link provided in it, alongside the Miro board content, can offer future authors of pictorials a resource, overview, and visual inspiration for their pictorials. Furthermore, we hope that future organisers of conferences will attempt to improve locating pictorials from ACM DL by tagging them as pictorials and also marking all awarded pictorials in the DL.

CONCLUSION

This pictorial contributes an overview of visual strategies employed in 271 pictorials published so far, through seven analytical themes. Each theme provides a set of perspectives to consider, complemented with examples of how authors have used these. The review focuses on how, rather than what design knowledge is communicated, but should not be seen as a delimitation of what pictorials can look like, but rather as an inspiration and an encouragement to keep up the visual experimentation with the format. The review offers newcomers to the format a starting point that goes beyond individual examples, as well as an online visual resource of the reviewed pictorials linked to ACM DL to easily explore interesting screenshot snippets from the corpus. Lastly, we discussed some of the challenges and potentials for the pictorial format that emerged from the visual review.
