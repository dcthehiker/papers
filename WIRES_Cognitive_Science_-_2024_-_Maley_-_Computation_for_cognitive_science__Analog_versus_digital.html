Abstract

Cognitive science was founded on the idea that the mind/brain can be understood in computational terms. While computational modeling in science is ubiquitous, cognitive science takes the stronger stance that the mind/brain literally performs computations. Moreover, performing computations is crucial to explaining what the mind/brain does, qua mind/brain. Unfortunately, most scientists fail to consider analog computation as a legitimate and theoretically useful type of computation in addition to digital computation; to the extent that analog computation is acknowledged, it is mostly based on a simplistic and incomplete understanding. Taking computation to consist of only one type (i.e., digital) while ignoring another, interestingly distinct type (i.e., analog) leads to an impoverished understanding of what it could mean for minds/ brains to compute. A full appreciation and understanding of analog computation-particularly in relation to digital computation-allows researchers to develop computational frameworks and hypotheses in new and exciting ways. Thus, somewhat counterintuitively, looking to the once-dominant computing paradigm of yesteryear can provide novel computational ways of thinking about the mind and brain.

1 | INTRODUCTION

One of the central tenets of classical cognitive science is the view that the mind/brain can be understood and explained as a computational system. This amounts to more than the claim that the mind/brain can be modeled computationally: virtually all scientific phenomena can be modeled computationally. For nearly all scientific fields X ,one can find departments,research groups,labs,and courses involved in "computational X" (e.g., computational astrophysics, computational geosciences, or computational meteorology). Roughly speaking, these all involve the use of computers and data processing techniques to model complex phenomena.
Importantly, cognitive science differs in hypothesizing that the operations of minds/brains can be understood not just as being amenable to computational modeling, but to computational explanation (Piccinini, 2007). Whereas
computational astrophysicists never claim that galaxies or planetary orbits - the target of their research-literally perform computations, cognitive scientists do claim that minds and brain literally compute, and this very fact is key to understanding what they do. Thus, if we are to take seriously that minds/brains are systems that literally perform computations-and are to be explained in terms of the computations that they literally perform-we must get clear about what computation is in the first place.

When thinking about computation in general, many researchers understand it to be no more and no less than digital computation. After all, this is the type of computation studied and theorized in computer science. However, cognitive scientists and neuroscientists deploy the notion of neural computation. Neural computation-also called neural coding-is sometimes considered digital, and sometimes analog (Clark & Häusser, 2006; Danchin & Fenton, 2022; Mochizuki &Shinomoto,2014). 1 The distinction between analog and digital in these discussions follows the general understanding that "analog" is synonymous with "continuous," and "digital" is synonymous with "discrete." This is unfortunate, because this leads to a rather impoverished view of analog computation. Although analog computation is quite often continuous, it is not necessarily so; moreover, focusing on only this particular feature obscures much more interesting features of analog computation.
To best understand the distinction between analog and digital computation, it helps to start with the difference between analog and digital representation. Many interesting connections between analog and digital representation can be made with other distinctions in cognitive science, such as the distinction between iconic and discursive representation (Block, 2023; Carey, 2009; Maley, 2023b), or the role of analog representation in particular cognitive tasks, particularly involving spatial memory and mental rotation (Kosslyn, 1983). Space constraints prevent any further discussion of these connections; we will focus on analog and digital computation—and the representations underlying them—here.
By looking more carefully at analog computation, particularly in terms of both its differences and similarities with digital computation, we can see that certain assumptions about the nature of computation in general do not hold for analog computation; they only hold for digital computation. The result is that a host of features that we have taken to be constitutive of computation are only specific to one (of at least two) separate types of computation.
To be sure, for almost all practical purposes, digital computers serve us better than the analog computers of the last century. However, for theoretical purposes, we should avail ourselves of all types of computation: it is an empirical question which type of computation is performed by the mind/brain; there is no a priori reason to consider only digital. Understanding analog computation, along with digital, allows cognitive scientists to think of computations in broader terms. At the same time, broadening "computation" to include the analog is not an ad hoc extension, but a principled one, based on a well-developed—albeit mostly forgotten—family of computational mechanisms.
In what follows, I will briefly describe analog computation, and show that its differences from digital computation are not captured by the simple distinction between continuous and discrete computation. I will then highlight some features of analog computation that are of particular interest in cognitive science.

2 | ANALOG COMPUTATION IN A NUTSHELL

The received view of how to distinguish analog computation from digital computation centers around the distinction between continuity and discreteness: analog computation is continuous, whereas digital computation is discrete. While it is true that analog computers frequently used continuous variables, this was not always the case: some analog computers used discrete variables, but this did not then make them digital. We will see an example below, but first let us get some basic ideas on the table.

2.1 | Analog representation

A better way to understand analog computation is via the manipulation of analog representations. The obvious next question is: what is an analog representation? Two views are on offer: the continuous conception and the mirroring conception (Beck, 2015). As the name suggests, the first takes analog representation to be essentially (and necessarily) continuous (Haugeland, 1981; Papayannopoulos, 2020). Because analog computers operate on analog representations, this is just the received view of analog computation. However, this view does not do justice to discrete, discontinuous analog representations, and thus discrete, discontinuous analog computers. The other view of analog representation, the mirroring conception, takes the essential feature of analog representation to be a "mirroring" between the representation and what is being represented (Beck, 2019; Lewis, 1971; Maley, 2011).
Some examples can help draw out the difference between these views. A mercury thermometer is a representation of temperature: temperature is represented by the height of a column of liquid, and both the temperature and height vary continuously (or close enough for practical purposes). On both views, this would count as an analog representation: everything is continuous, and the height of the liquid "mirrors" the magnitude of the temperature.
Now consider a similar thermometer that, for whatever reason, has a liquid that increases or decreases in discrete steps. Temperature is still represented by the height of the liquid, but now it is no longer continuous. Or consider a very large and slow hourglass that uses marbles, where one marble passes from the top to bottom every hour. Elapsed time is represented by the number of marbles in the lower part of the hourglass, but now both what is represented (number of hours elapsed) and what is doing the representing (quantity of marbles) are discrete.
These examples only count as analog on the mirroring conception because of the discreteness involved. However, the relationship between what is represented and what is doing the representing is the same: the one mirrors the other. Moreover, this discrete-yet analog-type of representation was actually used in analog computers, as well as many everyday devices we are familiar with. An analog clock, for example, does not become non-analog when the secondhand ticks in discrete steps.
We will look at more aspects of analog representation below, particularly relative to digital computation. For now, we simply note that, in order to best understand analog computation (and in order to understand discrete analog computation at all), we must use the mirroring conception, rather than the continuous conception, of analog representation.

2.2 | Computing with analog representations

Analog computers have been used for millennia-the earliest known example of some complexity is the Antikythera mechanism (Bruderer, 2020). Here, I will only mention those used in the twentieth century: mechanical, electromechanical, and electronic analog computers. Superficially, these machines seem quite different. Mechanical analog computers consisted of precisely machined components, such as rotating cylinders and linkages, moving in complex ways. Electronic analog computers, on the other hand, had no moving components aside from input and output, otherwise consisting entirely of electronic circuits. And in the middle were the electromechanical analog computers, which used both mechanical and electronic elements.
An example of an electromechanical analog computing component illustrates several of the important features of analog computers. The left side of Figure 1 shows a sketch of a step function generator, adapted from (Korn & Korn, 1952). This component has a rotating hand, much like the second hand of a clock, that makes (and breaks) contact with wires at different points along a resistor. When it makes contact, it completes a circuit, allowing current to flow. By having contact points with different resistances, and having break points where no current flows, this component generated a true, discrete, step function (right side of Figure 1).
The values of the step function are simply the magnitudes of the voltage. Unlike a digital computer, the voltages are not a code for the individual digits of a number. Instead, the voltages directly represent the values of numbers. In this example, the function's values are 20 , then 15.5 , then 4.5 , and so on, corresponding to the output of the mechanism being 20V ,then 15.5V ,then 4.5V ,and so on.

3 | DIFFERENCES BETWEEN ANALOG AND DIGITAL COMPUTATION

Many important subtleties about analog computation have been obscured because of the simplistic notion that "analog" just means "continuous" and "digital" just means "discrete." Some of these subtleties that are most interesting for cognitive science are highlighted here.

3.1 | Digits versus magnitudes

Whereas digital computers operate on digital representations, analog computers operate on analog representations. In both cases, the basic unit that the computing system manipulates is the representation of a number, but the way that the numbers are represented is fundamentally different (Maley, 2011).
Virtually all digital computers use binary (base-2) representations: numbers are represented via bits. This scheme is just like the familiar decimal (base-10) system, but with only two possible numerals per "place" than the 10 of the decimal system. In decimal,"107" is interpreted as (1×102)+(0×101)+(7×100) . In binary,that same number would be represented as "1101011",interpreted as (1×26)+(1×25)+(0×24)+(1×23)+(0×22)+(1×21)+(1×20) . Electronic computers use voltages to represent each individual digit,using a value such as 5V to represent a "1," and 0V to represent a " 0. " The particular voltages do not matter,as long as there are two separate values that the machine can distinguish (e.g.,a different machine might use negative 5V and positive 5V ).
In many applications, these digital representations will then represent other things, or be used as the parts of more complex representations. A particular number might represent a letter in a text document, part of the RGB value of a pixel, or an instruction of machine code. However, much of the basic architecture of digital computers (arithmetic and logical operations, memory addressing, and control) is founded on using binary representations of numbers.
Analog computers, on the other hand, use physical magnitudes to represent the magnitude of numbers. Instead of a digital representation,an analog computer would use 107V to represent the value 107 (or,instead of voltage,a length of 107mm ,or a mass of 107g ). Manipulating analog representations amounts to physically manipulating these magnitudes in a way that reflects how one wants to manipulate the magnitude they represent. For example, to add 17-107, we increase the voltage from 107 to 124V (or the length to 124mm ,etc.).
Of course, digital computers use physical magnitudes, too. However, the physical magnitudes of digital computers are used to represent individual numerals; the numerals in a particular sequence are the digital representation of the number in question. This difference is subtle, but important, and completely missed when we think of analog versus digital as simply the difference between continuous and discrete. Figure 2 illustrates this difference.
Note that when the value of the number being represented increases in the analog case, the magnitude doing the representing literally (i.e.,physically) increases: 124V is literally a larger voltage than 107V . In the digital case,there is only a different pattern of voltages: some of the voltages changed from 0 to 5V (and vice versa),and some did not change at all. Obviously, our interpretation of what these individual voltage changes represent increases, but physically, nothing corresponds to the increase as it does in the analog case. Interestingly, the analog representation is much more efficient in a case like this. The digital representation requires seven distinct circuit elements, whereas the analog requires just one. Moreover, manipulating the representation requires manipulating the entire sequence of circuit elements in the digital case. The analog case requires manipulating only the one (there are, of course, tradeoffs, such as precision and noise resilience).

3.2 | Marr's implementational and representational/algorithmic levels collapse

David Marr's highly influential methodological insight regarding how to understand and explain information processing systems involves distinguishing three distinct "levels": what he called the computational level, the representational/algorithmic level, and the implementational level (Marr, 1982). The computational level (CL) is concerned with the mathematical problem being solved and its appropriateness. The representational/algorithmic level (RAL) concerns how the variables are represented, and what algorithms are used to manipulate those variables. Finally, the implementational level (IL) concerns the physical structures that are used to make the representations and algorithms concrete.

In the case of a simple calculator, a CL analysis would specify that the function (or, one of the functions) of a calculator is to add integers. An RAL analysis would specify that the input and output representations are decimal (i.e., the display is in base-10), the internal representations are binary, and that the algorithm used is partial sums (starting from the least-significant digit). An IL analysis would specify that voltage values are mapped to binary digits, the circuitry of the digital logic design of the adder, and specify how input and output display values are converted to and from the internal binary representations (of course, each level may contain more elements: this is just an example of some of them).
In the case of an analog adder, the CL analysis would be the same as above: the function is to add integers. The RAL would specify that integers are represented by voltages, and that addition is performed by the mechanism that adds voltages. The IL would specify ... the same thing as the RAL. There is no distinction between the representation and the physical magnitudes that do that representing. Moreover, there is no distinction between a specification of how those magnitudes are so manipulated (given that they are physical magnitudes) and the physical specification of how that happens. The RAL and the IL thus collapse (Maley (2021) provides more details and a more detailed argument). This collapse is also illustrated in Figure 2, where the CL of both the analog and digital representations are the same, but the digital representation has an intermediate level between the CL and the IL (namely, the digital representation of the RAL) at the top of the Figure 2.

3.3 | No distinction between hardware and software

Because of the Marrian level collapse mentioned above, there is no distinction between hardware and software in ana- log computers. In this way they are like artificial neural networks: the computation happens because of the structure of the mechanism manipulating the activations (i.e., the connections and their weights in the case of artificial neural networks), rather than a separate algorithm run on hardware.
There are interesting conceptual issues regarding this fact about analog computation, as well as other computing paradigms that either collapse or blur the distinction between hardware and software. For instance, it is not clear that analog computers have "algorithms" at all; rather, they have mechanisms. Electronic analog computers can perform integration using an operational amplifier (op-amp), and mechanical analog computers can perform integration using a disk integrator. Digital computers, on the other hand, perform integration by using a variety of numerical algorithms, such as the Runge-Kutta method (see Figure 3).

Both the disk integrator and the Runge-Kutta method perform the same mathematical task: integrating a function. A CL analysis would therefore be the same. But understanding how that task is performed (i.e., the RAL analysis) in the analog case requires appealing to the particular disk integrator mechanism, whereas understanding how it is performed in the digital case requires appealing to the particular algorithm used. Thus, in the analog case, we must understand the physical magnitudes involved (i.e., the representations) and how they are manipulated; in this case, those magnitudes are rotations and left-right displacement. What is done by software in the digital case is done by physical mechanisms in analog case, blurring (or eliminating entirely) any distinction between software and hardware in analog computation.

4 | LESSONS FOR A GENERAL UNDERSTANDING OF COMPUTATION IN COGNITIVE SCIENCE

My hope is that it is clear how very different analog computation is from digital computation, beyond the simplistic "continuous-versus-discrete" dichotomy. A few points about the implications for computation in general are highlighted here.

4.1 | Computation does not necessarily involve algorithms

The operations performed by computations are sometimes governed by algorithms, but sometimes governed by mechanisms. Given a specification of the function to be computed (i.e., a CL analysis), a particular algorithm describes how that function is computed in the digital case, whereas a particular mechanism describes how that function is computed in the analog case.
When it comes to cognitive science, this suggests that it may be a mistake, or at least misguided, to look for the algorithms involved in computing some function (at least insofar as the computation may be an analog one). Instead, one should look for the mechanisms involved, where physical magnitudes of the mechanism are the representational vehicles for the computation involved. Unlike algorithms, mechanisms may not be discrete, step-by-countable-step, where each step is maximally simple, and with a well-defined stopping point (all of which are requirements of algorithms in computer science-think of how a Turing machine, or any computationally equivalent formalism, is defined).
This is not to say that a mathematical characterization is irrelevant; both analog and digital computations involve a mathematical characterization at the CL. But it is important to distinguish what is being computed (the particular function-i.e., the CL) from how it is being computed (which might be a mechanism or an algorithm, depending on the type of computation-i.e., the RAL).

4.2 | Computation is not necessarily medium/substrate independent

One of the benefits of digital computation is that it can be implemented in a variety of physical media. Abstract specifications of a program, from Turing machines to Python code, are completely independent of any particular substrate. This allows the same program to be run on all types of digital computers. Plus, algorithms can be analyzed independently of the hardware on which they are run (or independent from any particular physical considerations, for that matter).
For analog computers, the situation is different. Understanding the mechanism involved does require going into concrete, physical details: which magnitudes are relevant to the representations involved, and which physical processes manipulate those representations. Of course, there are limits: the disk integrator above could be made of steel, brass, wood, or ice-in that weak sense, the physical substrate does not matter. However, we do need to know that the physical magnitudes involved are rotations, displacements, angles, voltages, and so on. The mechanism may well be multiply realizable, but that is not medium-independent in the way that algorithms are typically taken to be (Polger & Shapiro, 2016).

4.3 | Computation does essentially involve representation

The only way to distinguish computational from non-computational processes is via the manipulation of representations. This is contentious: some understand "computation" to be merely the discrete-state dynamics of a physical system; some understand "computation" to be whatever can be done by a Turing machine. There is much to say about these alternatives, but when it comes to physical computation-particularly when it comes to making sense of whether natural systems, such as the mind/brain, literally compute (and not in a trivial sense of pancomputationalism, according to which everything computes)-we should focus on physical computational systems and how they work. And physical computation, when we include analog computation, essentially involves representations and their algorithmic or mechanistic manipulation.

5 | CONCLUSION

Does the mind/brain compute? Do particular neurons, or neural circuits, compute? These questions are, perhaps, most interesting if we can treat them as subject to empirical results, as opposed to simply a matter of perspective, opinion, or stipulation (Maley, 2022). This requires getting clear about what it is for something to compute. We miss an important type of computation-once the dominant paradigm of computation-when we limit our thinking to digital computation. By including analog computation, we can broaden our conception of what computation is more generally. At the same time, this broadening is not so expansive as to count everything as computing, at least when we understand computation to involve representation, either analog or digital (or maybe even something else), plus a mechanism or algorithm to systematically manipulate those representations. Whether some part or aspect of a physical system is a representation is, of course, a difficult question, and one that different cognitive scientists and neuroscientists may answer differently (Baker et al., 2022). But once we have determined that a system does use representations, then we can determine what types of representations they are, how those representations are manipulated (if they are), and then answer the question of whether the system literally performs computations.
Assumptions about the nature of computation in general simply do not hold when we consider computation in this broadened sense. There need not be a clear division between hardware and software; there need not be an explicit (or implicit) algorithm involved in the workings of the system; there need not be anything like a digital "code," where physical signals represent the numerals of digits. So, for example, perhaps the firing rate of a neuron is better thought of as representing the magnitude of a stimulus (i.e., an analog representation, whether discrete or continuous), rather than as a stream of bits; or perhaps not. But it is better to have at our conceptual disposal two different types of representation that could underwrite two different types of computation, rather than trying to fit everything into one
computational framework, or none at all. To make sense of how the mind/brain works, we need to use all available resources, including the framework of analog computation.
