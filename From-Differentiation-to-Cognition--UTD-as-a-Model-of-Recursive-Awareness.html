From Differentiation to Cognition: UTD as a Model of Recursive Awareness


Denys Spirin

ORCID: 0009-0004-7701-8417

May 10, 2025

Abstract


We propose the Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD) as a foundational framework for modeling cognition. UTD formalizes cognitive acts as structured differentiations within a hierarchy of categories Δn . Recursive fixpoints Dn+1(δ,δ)=In express stability, recognition, and self-reference. We show how perception, memory, attention, and self-awareness can be represented as acts within this hierarchy, compare UTD with major theories of consciousness, and outline implications for AI and cognitive architecture.

1 Introduction


Contemporary models of cognition—ranging from Global Workspace Theory (GWT) [1, 6], Integrated Information Theory (IIT) [22], to predictive processing [8, 5] and enactivist frameworks [26] have provided valuable insights into the architecture and dynamics of conscious experience. Yet, these models often share an implicit assumption: that the constituents of cognition (signals, representations, or processes) are already given, while the act by which they are distinguished is left unformalized.

The Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD), recently introduced as a categorical framework based on structured acts of distinction [20], offers a new perspective. A detailed formal account of the UTD hierarchy, including the topos-theoretic structure and key theorems, is presented in [20]. This paper builds on that foundation to explore cognitive implications. Instead of taking objects and relations as primitives, UTD constructs a hierarchy of categories Δn ,where each object is an act of differentiation -formalized as a triadic structure D1(a,b,α) -and morphisms preserve contextual structure across levels. Recursive differentiation,via Dn+1 ,allows the construction of higher-order distinctions.

In this paper, we explore the consequences of UTD for modeling cognition. We argue that cognition itself may be viewed as a recursive cascade of structured distinctions. Perception, inference, memory, and even self-awareness emerge not from fixed modules but from stability conditions within this hierarchy. We show that cognitive stability corresponds to fixpoints in the structure Dn+1(δ,δ)=In ,and that recursive self-reference emerges naturally as higher-order differentiation.





This approach opens a path toward a general theory of cognitive differentiation, applicable not only to human reasoning but also to artificial systems, conceptual design, and philosophical accounts of awareness.

2 Overview of the Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD)


The Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD) is a foundational framework in which all structure arises from acts of distinction. Rather than presupposing objects, processes, or representations, UTD begins with the notion that *to be is to be differentiated*. Each act of differentiation is formalized as a morphism within a recursive categorical hierarchy.

Let Δn be the category of n -th order differentiations. An object δ∈Δn represents an act Dn(a,b,α) : a distinction between a and b under aspect α . These acts are contextual and do not require pre-existing entities - they are ontologically primary.

The recursive architecture is governed by fixpoint stability:

(1)Dn+1(δ,δ)=In

where In is the identity in Δn . This expresses that the act δ is recognized as itself — a condition corresponding to memory, reflection, or coherent self-reference.

Axioms of UTD include:

  • Primacy of distinction: Only acts of differentiation are ontologically primitive.

  • Recursive structuring: Each level Δn+1 is defined over objects and morphisms in Δn .

  • Stability by reflection: A distinction is stable if it maps onto itself under recursive differentiation.

  • Contextual emergence: Differentiations are interpreted within local categorical contexts Cn .

These axioms replace static assumptions about mental content with dynamic conditions on the stability and coherence of structured difference. The theory allows for a unified modeling of perception,attention,and self-awareness as transitions within the hierarchy Δn .

In what follows, we explore how UTD's recursive structure naturally captures core cognitive functions, including temporality, memory, and introspective loop closure.

3 From Perception to Differentiation


We begin by interpreting perceptual experience as a structured act of distinction. In UTD [20], the primitive unit of structure is a first-order differentiation:

D1(a,b,α)=δ1∈Δ1



where a and b are sensory states or signals,and α is an aspect-such as "color","shape", "location",or "intensity"-that defines the mode of comparison. The result, δ1 ,is a structured relation embedded in the contextual category C1 ,whose objects are perceptual contexts.

This differs fundamentally from classical input-output models: UTD treats perception not as an extraction of features, but as a construction of structured differences. Each differentiation act is context-sensitive, and its morphisms preserve the internal coherence of perceptual space.

Higher-order perception arises when such acts are themselves compared. For instance, suppose δ1=D1(a,b,α) and δ2=D1(c,d,α) : the structure

D2(δ1,δ2)=δ2′∈Δ2

represents a meta-perceptual act: it compares two distinctions under shared aspect α , establishing a second-order differentiation.

At this level, UTD captures what is often called "pattern recognition" or "feature integration" in cognitive science [24] - but formalized through recursive categorification. The contextual category C2 contains not raw perceptual signals,but structured patterns of differentiation.

As this process continues,we obtain D3(δ2′,δ3′) ,and so on. The hierarchy Δn is not a stack of modules, but a reflective ladder: each level internalizes the structure of the previous one through acts of comparison.

In this framework, perception becomes inherently temporal and recursive. Memory is not stored data, but stability across differentiation levels. Recognition is the emergence of a stable fixed point Dn+1(δ,δ)=In ,and surprise is its failure.

Thus, UTD reframes perception as a process of active differentiation-not of states, but of differences themselves.

3.1 Computing Differentiation Acts


To make UTD applicable to cognitive modeling and artificial systems, we describe how to compute differentiation acts Dn . The computation of D1(a,b,α) involves evaluating the aspect α on a pair of inputs a,b ,producing a structured distinction δ1∈Δ1 . Higher-order differentiations Dn+1(δ1,δ2) recursively compare acts from Δn ,leveraging the contextual structure of Cn .

Formally,let X be a set of sensory inputs, A a set of aspects,and Cn a category of contexts at level n . The algorithm for computing D1 is as follows:

  1. Input: Elements a,b∈X ,aspect α∈A ,and context c∈Ob(C1) .

  1. Evaluate aspect: Compute α(a,b) ,which may be a numerical value (e.g.,distance), a logical relation (e.g., equality), or a structural comparison (e.g., shape similarity).

  1. Check applicability: If α is undefined for(a,b)in context c ,return ⊥ (undefined). Otherwise,construct δ1=D1(a,b,α) .





  1. Assign to Δ1 : Embed δ1 as a presheaf over C1 ,mapping context c to the structured distinction.

For higher-order differentiation Dn+1 :

  1. Input: Acts δ1,δ2∈Δn ,context c∈Ob(Cn+1) .

  1. Compare structures: Evaluate the structural difference between δ1 and δ2 over all contexts in Cn ,using a canonical aspect defined by the recursive structure of UTD (e.g., morphism compatibility).

  1. Construct δ′ : Form δ′=Dn+1(δ1,δ2)∈Δn+1 ,embedding it as a presheaf over Cn+1 .

The computational complexity of D1 depends on the complexity of evaluating α ,typically O(f(|X|,|α|)) ,where f is the complexity of α . For Dn+1 ,the complexity is exponential in n,O(2n⋅g(|X|,|A|)) ,due to the hierarchical growth of Δn ,where g bounds the representation of acts 20 .

Example: Face Recognition


Consider a scenario where a system recognizes a face in a crowd. Let a be the visual input of a face, b the background (other faces or noise),and α the aspect "facial contour" (e.g., edge detection of eyes, nose, mouth). The computation proceeds as follows:

  • Level Δ1 : Compute D1(a,b,facial contour)=δ1 ,where δ1 is the extracted contour of the face,embedded in context C1 (e.g.,lighting conditions,image resolution). For example,if α is implemented as an edge-detection algorithm [4], δ1 is a vector of contour features.

  • Level Δ2 : Compare δ1 with a stored act δ2=D1(a′,b′ ,facial contour),representing a known face from memory. Compute D2(δ1,δ2)=δ2′ ,which evaluates the similarity (e.g.,Euclidean distance between feature vectors) in context C2 (e.g.,memory associations). If δ2′ indicates high similarity,recognition occurs.

  • Stability: Check D3(δ2′,δ2′)=I2 ,ensuring the recognition is stable (e.g.,the similarity holds across multiple contexts, such as varying angles or lighting). If stable, the face is confirmed as familiar; otherwise, a surprise (non-recognition) occurs.

This example illustrates how UTD models perception as a recursive process, with computations grounded in structured distinctions. In an AI system, D1 could be implemented via convolutional neural networks for feature extraction [15],while D2 involves comparing feature vectors, with stability verified through a threshold on similarity metrics.

4 Cognitive Stability as Recursive Fixpoint


Cognition is not merely the processing of external stimuli; it involves the construction and maintenance of internally coherent patterns over time. In UTD, such coherence corresponds to the notion of stability, defined formally as a recursive fixpoint condition:





(2)Dn+1(δ,δ)=In

Here, δ is a structured act of differentiation in Δn ,and In is the terminal object in Δn+1 , interpreted as a trivial or identity differentiation at level n+1 . This fixpoint condition expresses that δ remains invariant when reflected upon-it differentiates itself from itself and yields no new distinction. In cognitive terms, such a condition corresponds to the recognition of a pattern, the convergence of attention, or the achievement of conceptual fixity.

This idea may be interpreted in several ways:

  • In perception,a stable δ corresponds to a perceptual pattern that is reinforced upon further exposure (e.g., recognizing a familiar face [14]).

  • In memory, stability manifests as a trace or schema that persists across retrieval contexts [19].

  • In reasoning, a stable inference is one that reproduces itself under internal scrutiny (i.e., meta-cognition [16]).

Stability thus plays a role analogous to convergence in dynamical systems or fixpoints in lambda calculus. But unlike those systems, UTD's fixpoint is categorical and reflective: it arises not from iteration over values, but from the internal logic of structured differentiation [13].

Topos-theoretic Framing


Since each Δn is an elementary topos [13],it comes equipped with a subobject classifier Ωn , a terminal object Tn ,and categorical constructions such as pullbacks and exponentials. The stability of δ can be reinterpreted via the characteristic morphism χδ:δ→Ωn ,where

(3)χδ=⊤n if and only if Dn+1(δ,δ)=In

This connects cognitive fixpoints to logical truth within the internal logic of Δn . A stable cognitive act is one whose self-reflection projects to a "true" morphism in the topos-the internal verification that the act coheres with itself.

5 Self-awareness as Higher-order Differentiation


The emergence of self-awareness presents a challenge to many cognitive models: how can a system not only process stimuli, but also reflect on its own processing? Within the UTD framework [20], self-awareness arises naturally as a consequence of higher-order differentiation.

Let δ∈Δn be a cognitive act-a perception,inference,or internal state. Then Dn+1(δ,δ′) compares δ with another act δ′ ,forming a second-order differentiation. In particular,when δ′=δ ,the expression Dn+1(δ,δ) captures the system’s capacity to differentiate its own act.

But this self-comparison is only meaningful when it becomes stable, i.e., when:

(5)Dn+1(δ,δ)=In

In such a case,the system identifies δ as internally coherent under recursive inspection-it not only performs an act, but recognizes it as an act. This is the minimal condition for self-awareness [9].

The Reflective Ladder


A single fixed-point is not sufficient to explain the richness of introspective cognition. Rather, self-awareness in UTD is modeled by an ascending chain of fixpoints:

(6)Dn+1(δn,δn)=In,Dn+2(δn+1,δn+1)=In+1,…

Each step in this ladder represents not merely recursion, but reflective stabilization: the capacity to distinguish one's own distinctions, to track them across levels, and to consolidate a stable cognitive identity.

This mirrors developmental and phenomenological models of selfhood [28], where the minimal self (as pre-reflective embodied presence) precedes the narrative or meta-cognitive self.

Examples of Recursive Self-differentiation


  • Mirror recognition: The subject sees a reflection and matches the perceptual act to its own motor or proprioceptive schema,establishing D2(δimage ,δbody ) [10].

  • Introspective report: A verbal report like `"I am thinking about this problem"` encodes a stable higher-order differentiation between thought-content and meta-thought [16].





  • Meta-attention: The act of noticing that one is distracted involves recognizing instability and attempting to restore a fixpoint [17].

In each of these cases, what emerges is not an abstract "self" but a pattern of stability across levels of distinction. UTD thus avoids reifying selfhood, treating it instead as a dynamically maintained configuration within the hierarchy of Δn .

6 Comparison with Existing Models


The Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD) [20] offers a structural and recursive foundation for cognition that contrasts with, and complements, several prominent models in the contemporary cognitive sciences. In this section, we examine its alignment and divergence with three major frameworks: Integrated Information Theory (IIT) [22], Global Workspace Theory (GWT) [1, 6], and Predictive Processing (PP) [8, 5].

Integrated Information Theory (IIT)


IIT posits that consciousness corresponds to the amount of integrated information Φ within a system [22]. While this measure is mathematically formalized in terms of causal structures, it lacks a direct account of how distinctions arise and persist. UTD addresses this gap by focusing not on integration per se, but on the recursive structure of differentiation that makes integration meaningful.

Rather than quantifying Φ ,UTD provides a scaffold of Δn levels,within which stable fixpoints Dn+1(δ,δ)=In serve as the cognitive correlates of informational integration. This offers a possible bridge: integration in IIT corresponds to stable recursive differentiation in UTD.

Global Workspace Theory (GWT)


GWT models consciousness as the broadcasting of information from local processors into a global workspace [1, 6]. While this framework captures the architecture of access and attention, it leaves the content and structure of cognitive acts unspecified.

In UTD,each Δn functions as a structured cognitive workspace,and morphisms between levels formalize precisely the flow of structured information. The "globality" in GWT corresponds to the preservation of coherence across morphisms δ:Δn→Δn+1 ,while "access" reflects the pullback condition for simultaneous co-stability of multiple acts.

Moreover, UTD refines the GWT model by replacing a centralised workspace with a reflective hierarchy, where stability is achieved not by central control but by mutual coherence across levels of structured acts.

Predictive Processing (PP)


Predictive processing views cognition as the minimization of prediction error via hierarchical generative models [8, 5]. Perception, action, and learning are framed as updates to probabilistic expectations. This view resonates with recent work by Trapp et al. [23], who propose that intelligence consists in the capacity to flexibly regulate prediction precision-a dynamic analogue of the fixpoint condition Dn+1(δ,δ)=In in UTD.





UTD resonates with this view, but with a key shift: what is updated is not a value or belief, but a configuration of distinctions. Prediction corresponds to the expectation of stability: if Dn+1(δ,δ)=In holds,the act δ is maintained. A prediction error is detected as a failure of this condition.

In this sense, PP and UTD are complementary: PP models the dynamics of expectation, UTD provides a structural framework in which such expectations are encoded as acts of recursive differentiation.

Summary


Where IIT measures, GWT organizes, and PP anticipates, UTD structures. Its focus on the internal logic of cognitive acts-via categories, morphisms, and fixpoints-allows a unified treatment of perception, memory, attention, and self-reference. Rather than competing with existing models, UTD reframes their mechanisms in a formally coherent ontological substrate.

7 Implications for Cognitive Architecture


The recursive and categorical formulation of cognition in UTD [20] opens a pathway toward a new class of cognitive architectures. Unlike traditional models built around modules, buffers, or neural maps, a UTD-based architecture centers on structured acts of differentiation and the morphisms between them.

Hierarchy of Differentiation Layers


Each level Δn in the UTD hierarchy corresponds to a layer of cognitive processing:

  • Δ1 : raw perceptual differentiations (sensory distinctions based on simple aspects);

  • Δ2 : comparative integration of percepts - pattern recognition,basic memory traces;

  • Δ3 : reflective configurations-contextual associations,schema activation;

  • Δ4 and higher: introspective monitoring,conceptual abstraction,self-awareness.

This recursive layering replaces the classical separation between "low-level" and "high-level" cognition. All levels are structurally unified by Dn and share the same internal logic of stability and morphism preservation.

Cognitive Operations as Functors


Operations such as attention, inference, or recall can be modeled as functorial mappings between subcategories of Δn . For example,a focused attention process may be represented as a subfunctor F:C→Δn ,where C is a selection of stable differentiations. Inference may be modeled as a morphism-preserving transformation that establishes co-stability across distinct domains.





Because all objects in Δn are themselves structured acts,operations are always internal: there is no separation between data and control-only recursive structure.

Memory as Structural Stability


Rather than storing values, UTD models memory as the persistence of fixpoints across layers:

(7)Dn+1(δ,δ)=In and δ∈Fix(Δn)

Such fixed-points may be dynamically updated, re-evaluated, or forgotten via destabilization. This enables a model of working memory as temporarily stabilized differentiation, and long-term memory as persistent categorical fixpoints [19].

Neurobiological Correlates


To ground UTD in biological cognition, we map its categorical structures to neural mechanisms,linking the hierarchy Δn ,differentiation acts Dn ,and stability conditions to brain processes. The brain's hierarchical organization and recursive dynamics provide a natural substrate for UTD's framework.

  • Level Δ1 : Sensory Processing. First-order differentiations D1(a,b,α) correspond to feature detection in primary sensory cortices. For example,in visual processing, a (a visual stimulus) and b (background) are differentiated by neurons in V1 (primary visual cortex) based on aspects like edge orientation or contrast (α) [12]. The context C1 reflects local receptive fields and sensory input conditions.

  • Level Δ2 : Associative Integration. Second-order differentiations D2(δ1,δ2) align with integration in associative areas, such as the fusiform face area (FFA) for face recognition [14] or the superior temporal sulcus for auditory pattern integration. These regions compare sensory distinctions (δ1,δ2) to form patterns,with contexts C2 representing cross-modal or memory-based associations.

  • Stability and Synaptic Plasticity. The stability condition Dn+1(δ,δ)=In mirrors synaptic plasticity mechanisms, such as long-term potentiation (LTP) in the hippocampus [2],which stabilizes neural representations. A stable act δ corresponds to a neural ensemble that maintains consistent firing patterns across contexts, reinforced by Heb-bian learning [11].

  • Pullback and Co-activation. The pullback structure, modeling co-activation of acts, reflects synchronized activity across brain regions. For instance, associative memory involves co-activation of hippocampal and cortical neurons, mediated by theta rhythms in thalamo-cortical loops [3], ensuring joint stability of related distinctions (e.g., a melody and a visual memory).





  • Higher Levels and Self-awareness. Levels Δ3 and above,associated with reflection and self-awareness, map to the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and default mode network (DMN) [18]. The recursive ladder Dn+1(δn,δn)=In corresponds to meta-cognitive monitoring in the PFC, where neural circuits reflect on their own activity, supported by feedback loops with the anterior cingulate cortex [7].

Example: Face Recognition


Revisiting the face recognition example (Section 2.1), we map the UTD process to neural correlates:

  • Level Δ1 : The act D1(a,b,facial contour)=δ1 is implemented by V1 neurons detecting edges in the visual input a (a face) against b (background) [12]. The context C1 includes lighting and contrast, processed by early visual areas.

  • Level Δ2 : The comparison D2(δ1,δ2)=δ2′ occurs in the FFA,where δ1 (current face features) is matched against δ2 (stored face template) via pattern completion [14]. The context C2 involves episodic memory traces in the hippocampus [21].

  • Stability: Stability D3(δ2′,δ2′)=I2 is achieved through LTP in hippocampal-cortical circuits [2], ensuring the recognized face persists across contexts (e.g., different angles). Theta oscillations synchronize FFA and hippocampal activity [3], reinforcing the fixpoint.

This mapping suggests that UTD's categorical hierarchy aligns with the brain's recursive architecture, from sensory processing to meta-cognition. Future work could explore how neural oscillations (e.g., gamma, theta [3]) encode morphism preservation or how synaptic plasticity supports the topos-theoretic logic of Δn .

Applications to Artificial Intelligence


UTD's recursive and categorical framework offers a novel approach to designing AI systems that mimic cognitive processes such as perception, memory, and self-awareness [20]. Unlike traditional AI architectures such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [15] or transformers [27] which rely on fixed layers or attention mechanisms, a UTD-based AI would operate on structured acts of differentiation, dynamically constructing a hierarchy of distinctions.

In this model, an AI system processes inputs as follows:

  • Input Processing (Δ1) : Sensory inputs (e.g.,images,text) are differentiated into acts δ1=D1(a,b,α) ,where α defines task-specific aspects (e.g.,edge detection for images, token similarity for text).

  • Pattern Recognition (Δ2) : Higher-order differentiations D2(δ1,δ2) compare current acts with stored templates, enabling recognition or classification.

  • Stability and Learning: The system checks for stability Dn+1(δ,δ)=In ,updating its internal hierarchy when stability fails (e.g., encountering novel inputs). This process mimics learning without external labels, as stability is an intrinsic property.





  • Reflection (Δ3+) : Higher levels enable meta-processing,such as detecting inconsis-

tencies or reflecting on the system's own decisions, akin to self-awareness.

This approach contrasts with CNNs, which extract features through fixed convolutions [15],

and transformers, which rely on attention to weigh input tokens [27]. UTD's strength lies in

its ability to adaptively construct distinctions without predefined architectures, potentially

enabling more flexible and generalizable AI systems.

Future AI implementations could extend this framework to natural language processing (e.g., differentiating semantic structures) or robotics (e.g., sensorimotor distinctions), leveraging UTD's flexibility to model recursive cognition across domains.

Toward an Artificial Differentiator


A practical cognitive system based on UTD would not operate on symbols or neurons, but on structured differentiations. Each input is transformed into δ∈Δ1 ,which propagates through higher layers via Dn+1(δi,δj) ,seeking local and global stability.

Such a system would:

  • adaptively construct its own internal hierarchy of meaning;

  • evaluate stability without external labels or rewards;

  • detect novelty as failure of fixpoints;

  • reflect upon its own acts as structural entities.

This suggests a new direction for AI: not as symbolic reasoning or deep networks [15], but as recursive differentiation within a categorical substrate.

8 Conclusion


In this paper, we proposed the Universal Theory of Differentiation (UTD) [20] as a foundational framework for modeling cognition. By treating acts of differentiation-not objects, symbols, or signals- as the primitive units of cognitive structure, UTD offers a new categorical language for understanding perception, memory, inference, and self-awareness.

Each category Δn models a layer of structured distinctions,with morphisms preserving contextual coherence. Recursive applications of differentiation Dn+1 enable the system to construct meta-levels of analysis, yielding a reflective hierarchy where cognitive fixpoints encode stability, recognition, and self-reference.

We demonstrated how UTD aligns with and reframes existing models: providing structure for IIT [22], internal logic for GWT [1, 6], and differentiation dynamics for predictive processing [8, 5]. We then outlined the implications for cognitive architecture: a system built not from symbol manipulation or neural activation, but from acts of structured distinction and their transformations.





The notion of consciousness, in this view, is not an emergent phenomenon atop information flow-but a recursive stabilization of distinctions within a stratified logical space. The "self" is not a substance, but a configuration of coherently differentiated acts [28].

Future work will extend this framework toward implementation: simulating agents that differentiate and stabilize their internal categories, and exploring whether such systems can exhibit behaviors traditionally associated with understanding, creativity, or autonomy.

UTD does not explain away consciousness. It repositions it: not as a mystery, but as a structure. Not as a node, but as a process of recursive differentiation. Not as a thing-but as the coherence of distinction itself.
